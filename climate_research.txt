================================================================
CLIMATE CHANGE - COMPREHENSIVE ANALYSIS 2024
================================================================

SECTION 1: TEMPERATURE AND SEA LEVEL DATA

NASA and NOAA data confirm that 2023 was the warmest year on record since global temperature tracking began in 1880. Global average temperatures exceeded pre-industrial levels by 1.48°C (2.66°F), approaching the critical 1.5°C threshold established by the Paris Agreement.

The rate of global warming has accelerated significantly, with temperatures rising 0.32°C per decade since 1981 compared to 0.08°C per decade from 1880-1980. The 10 warmest years on record have all occurred since 2010.

Sea levels have risen 8-9 inches (21-24 cm) since 1880, with the rate accelerating to 3.6 mm per year (1.4 inches per decade) as measured by satellite altimetry. This represents a doubling of the rate observed in the 1990s (1.8 mm/year).

SECTION 2: POLAR ICE AND GLACIERS

Greenland lost an average of 279 billion tons of ice per year between 1993 and 2019, while Antarctica lost approximately 148 billion tons annually during the same period. Combined, these ice sheets contribute 60% of observed sea level rise.

Arctic sea ice extent has declined by 13% per decade since 1979, with 2023 recording the sixth-lowest minimum extent on record. The oldest and thickest ice has declined by 95% over the past 35 years.

Mountain glaciers worldwide lost 1.2 trillion tons of ice in 2023 alone, the largest single-year loss on record. The European Alps could lose 90% of their glacier volume by 2100 at current emission rates.

SECTION 3: RENEWABLE ENERGY TRANSITION

Solar energy costs have decreased by 89% over the past decade, from $0.28/kWh in 2014 to $0.03/kWh in 2024, making solar the cheapest electricity source in history according to Lazard's Levelized Cost of Energy Analysis.

Wind power now provides 8% of US electricity generation, up from 2% in 2010. Global wind capacity reached 837 GW in 2023, with China (40%), USA (16%), and Germany (8%) leading installations.

Renewable energy accounted for 30% of global electricity generation in 2023, with hydropower (16%), wind (7%), solar (5%), and biomass (2%). The International Energy Agency projects renewables will reach 50% of global generation by 2030.

SECTION 4: CARBON EMISSIONS AND ATMOSPHERIC CO2

Global CO2 emissions reached 37.4 billion tons in 2023, an increase of 1.1% from 2022. The top emitters are China (31%), United States (14%), India (7%), and EU (7%). Per capita emissions vary dramatically: US (14.7 tons/person), China (8.5 tons/person), India (2.5 tons/person).

Atmospheric CO2 concentration reached 423 ppm in 2024, the highest in 3 million years. Pre-industrial levels were 280 ppm. The current rate of increase is 2.5 ppm per year, faster than any period in the last 50,000 years.

SECTION 5: IMPACTS ON ECOSYSTEMS

Ocean acidification has increased by 30% since the Industrial Revolution, with surface ocean pH dropping from 8.21 to 8.10. This change threatens coral reefs, shellfish, and plankton that form the base of marine food webs.

The Great Barrier Reef has experienced six mass bleaching events since 1998, including four in the last decade. Coral cover has declined by 50% since 1985, threatening 1,500 fish species and tourism worth $6.4 billion annually.

SECTION 6: POLICY AND INTERNATIONAL AGREEMENTS

The Paris Agreement includes commitments from 195 countries to limit warming to well below 2°C, preferably 1.5°C. Current national pledges put the world on track for 2.7°C warming by 2100, far above the target.

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) represents the largest US climate investment in history at $369 billion, projected to reduce US emissions by 40% below 2005 levels by 2030.

Sources: NASA Goddard Institute, NOAA Climate Report, IPCC Sixth Assessment Report, International Energy Agency (IEA), Lazard Analysis, World Meteorological Organization
